1 Calculational Proofs

We advocate calculational proofs in which reasoning is goal directed and justified by simple axiomatic laws that can be checked syntactically rather than semantically.

\begin{calc} x \step{ reason for x = y } y \step[\leq]{ reason for y < z } z \end{calc}

2 Intro

  1. Discrete Mathematics includes logic (calculational reasoning), (data) sets, functions, relations, graphs, inductive types, and more.

    Conscious and fluent use of the language of (discrete) mathematics is the foundation for precise specification and rigorous reasoning in Computer Science and Software Engineering

  2. Goal: Understand the mechanics of mathematical expressions and proof.

  3. Propositional: Statements that can be either true or false; not numbers.

    Predicate: Propositional statement about some subjects.

  4. Calculus: Formalised reasoning through calculation.

    ‚ÄòHand wavy‚Äô English arguments tend to favour case analysis —considering what could happen in each possible scenario— which increases exponentially with each variable; in contrast, equality-based calculation is much simpler since it delegates intricate case analysis into codifed algebraic laws.

    E.g., Portia's Suitor's Dilemma has 4 unknowns, each being either true or false, and so has \(2^4\) many possible scenarios to consider. Whereas a calculation solving the problem can be formed in less than 10 super simple lines.

  5. CalcCheck: A proof checker for the logic of ‚ÄúA Logical Approach to Discrete Math‚Äù (‚ÄòLADM‚Äô).

    • It checks your arguments in a notation similar to that of the book.
    • You can check your work before handing it in.
    • You can formalise your own theorems from other books and check them —unlimited exercises!

    Control + Alt + Enter to check a cell.

    • CalcCheckDoc - Documentation including syntax hints and theorem lists

3 CalcCheck Usage Hints

3.1 Calculational Steps

For clarifying terminology, here is an example calculation step: 

    x + 2
  =⟨ “Symmetry of +” ⟩
    2 + x

The part between the hint brackets ‚Äú‚ü®‚Äù and ‚Äú‚ü©‚Äù is called the hint for this calculation step, and it serves to justify that the expression before (x + 2) is related via the relation denoted by the calculation operator (=) with the expression after (2 + x). So, altogether, in this case the hint ‚ÄúSymmetry of +‚Äù is expected to justify the proposition x + 2 = 2 + x (and it does).

  • Use \< and \> to enter the hint delimiters ‚ü® and ‚ü©.

In general, a hint may contain several hint items; for now, only one hint item will be allowed per hint, and the only kinds of hint you can use here are:

Theorem references: These can be

  • theorem names in pretty double quotes, such as ‚ÄúUnary minus‚Äù,
    • Enter the quotes with \ldq + TAB and \rdq + TAB, for ‚Äòl‚Äôeft and ‚Äòr‚Äôight ‚Äòd‚Äôouble ‚Äòq‚Äôuotes.
    • After starting a theorem name, press TAB to see possible completions.
    • E.g., both \ldq + TAB + Dist + TAB and "Dist + TAB yield the completion list for theorems whose names begin with Dist, such as distributivity laws.
  • theorem numbers, which are always enclosed in parentheses, such as (15.5b)

  • combinations of both, which narrow down the selection of theorems to their intersection; this is currently mostly useful to disambiguate while still documenting the proof via the theorem name: 

    “Distributivity of · over -” (15.29b)

    In that particular case, omitting the number would still be accepted, but the system then may explore the unused (15.29a) before it is successful with (15.29b).

  • Basic facts such as: Fact `5 ¬∑ 6 = 30` . The expression between the backticks here has to consist entirely of literals and operators for which built-in evaluation is enabled. In particular, expressions containing variables are not accepted for Fact; writing Fact `x ¬∑ 0 = 0` will only get you an unjustified step.

3.2 The Shape of Calculations

Below is an exploratory calculation that begins with a richly structured known assumption and proceeds with the sole goal of simplification. Happily, the exploration ends somewhere fruitful and so we decide to name the result of the calculation using the (case sensitive clause) This proves: Theorem ‚Äú‚ü™name‚ü´‚Äù: ‚ü™result‚ü´ —with no indentation. However, the exploration may not end anywhere useful, and so we may keep it around for further study but need not name it; as such This prove ‚Ä¶ is optional. 

Calculation:
    S ≡ (S ≡ ¬ G)  — This is “Inscription on silver casket”
  ≡⟨ “Reflexivity of ≡” ⟩
    S ≡ S ≡ ¬ G
  ≡⟨ “Symmetry of ≡” ⟩
    ¬ G
  ≡⟨ “Inscription on gold casket” ⟩
    ¬ ¬ gc
  ≡⟨ “Double negation” ⟩
    gc
This proves: Fact “location of the portrait”: gc
